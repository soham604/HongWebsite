CMHC Multifamily Financing: The Complete Guide for Quebec Investors

What Is CMHC Multifamily Insurance?

Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation (CMHC) offers mortgage loan insurance for rental properties with 5 or more units. This insurance protects lenders against borrower default, which allows them to offer significantly better terms than conventional financing.

For Quebec investors, CMHC-insured financing is one of the most powerful tools available. It reduces your required down payment, extends your amortization period, and typically offers lower interest rates than conventional mortgages.

Eligibility Requirements

To qualify for CMHC multifamily insurance, your property must meet several criteria:

  • Minimum 5 residential rental units — this is the threshold that separates multifamily from residential
  • Property must be in Canada and meet CMHC's physical condition standards
  • Minimum Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR) of 1.1x — meaning the property's net operating income must cover at least 110% of the mortgage payments
  • Environmental assessment may be required depending on the property
  • Property appraisal from a CMHC-approved appraiser

In Quebec specifically, properties must comply with the Régie du logement regulations, and rental income projections must reflect Quebec market rents.

Down Payment: As Low as 5%

This is the headline benefit. With CMHC insurance, you can acquire a multifamily property with as little as 5% down. Compare that to conventional financing, which typically requires 20-25% down for the same property.

On a $1,000,000 six-plex, that is the difference between putting down $50,000 and $200,000. That freed-up capital can go toward your next acquisition or property improvements.

Amortization: Up to 50 Years

Through the CMHC MLI Select program, qualifying properties can access amortization periods of up to 50 years. Standard CMHC-insured multifamily loans typically offer up to 40 years, and MLI Select extends this further for properties that meet specific social, accessibility, or climate compatibility criteria.

Longer amortization means lower monthly payments, which improves your cash flow from day one. A 50-year amortization versus a 25-year amortization can reduce your monthly payment by roughly 30%.

MLI Select Scoring: Properties earn points for affordability (rents below median market rent), accessibility (barrier-free units), and energy efficiency (GHG reductions). Higher scores unlock longer amortization and lower insurance premiums.

Insurance Premiums

CMHC charges an insurance premium based on the loan-to-value ratio and the amortization period. Premiums typically range from 2.6% to 6.15% of the loan amount. This premium can be added to the mortgage, so you do not need to pay it upfront out of pocket.

While the premium adds to your total cost, the math usually works in your favour: the lower interest rate you receive with CMHC insurance often more than offsets the premium cost over the life of the loan.

Interest Rates

Because CMHC insurance eliminates lender risk, insured multifamily mortgages typically carry interest rates that are 50 to 150 basis points lower than conventional rates. On a million-dollar mortgage, that can save you $5,000 to $15,000 per year in interest.

Quebec-Specific Considerations

  • Régie du logement: Quebec's rental regulations affect how quickly you can increase rents, which impacts your proforma projections
  • Municipal taxes: Property transfer taxes (welcome tax) in Quebec are calculated on a progressive scale and can be significant on multifamily transactions
  • Language requirements: Documentation and tenant communications in Quebec must comply with language laws
  • Energy efficiency: Quebec's climate programs can complement MLI Select scoring for better terms

When CMHC Makes Sense

CMHC-insured financing is not always the right choice. It works best when you want to maximize leverage, preserve capital for multiple acquisitions, or when the property meets MLI Select criteria for extended amortization. If you are buying a property that needs significant renovation, conventional financing with a value-add strategy may be more appropriate.

The best approach is to run the numbers both ways — CMHC-insured versus conventional — and see which path delivers better returns for your specific situation.

How to Build a Proforma for Your First Multifamily Property

What Is a Proforma and Why Does It Matter?

A proforma is a financial projection that estimates a property's income, expenses, and cash flow. It is the single most important document in your financing application. Lenders use it to determine whether your property can service the debt, and they will scrutinize every line.

A well-built proforma does not just help you get approved — it helps you make better investment decisions. If the numbers do not work on paper, they will not work in reality.

Step 1: Calculate Gross Potential Revenue

Start with the total rental income the property could generate if every unit were occupied at market rent for the full year.

  • List each unit with its current rent and the market rent for comparable units in the area
  • Include other income such as parking, laundry, storage lockers, or any other revenue the property generates
  • In Quebec, be realistic about rent increases — the Tribunal administratif du logement publishes annual guidelines that limit how much you can raise rents on existing tenants

Step 2: Apply Vacancy and Bad Debt Allowance

No property stays 100% occupied year-round. Lenders expect you to account for vacancy, typically 3% to 5% of gross revenue for well-located Quebec multifamily properties. If the property is in a secondary market or has higher turnover, use 5% or more.

This gives you your Effective Gross Income (EGI) — the revenue you can realistically expect to collect.

Step 3: Itemize Operating Expenses

This is where most first-time investors make mistakes. Lenders will reject a proforma with unrealistic expense assumptions. Key expense categories include:

  • Municipal and school taxes: Verify the exact amounts from the municipal tax bill. In Quebec, these are publicly available through the role d'évaluation
  • Insurance: Get an actual quote for the specific property. Multifamily insurance costs vary significantly based on building age, construction type, and location
  • Heating and utilities: If the landlord pays heating (common in older Quebec buildings), this is a major expense line. Budget $800–$1,500 per unit annually depending on the building
  • Maintenance and repairs: Budget 3–5% of gross revenue for ongoing maintenance. Older buildings need more
  • Property management: Even if you self-manage, lenders typically want to see 3–5% of EGI allocated here. If you hire a manager, the actual cost is usually 4–7%
  • Snow removal and landscaping: A Quebec-specific line item that is often overlooked. Budget $1,500–$4,000+ depending on property size
  • Common area electricity: Hallway lighting, exterior lights, laundry room — typically $500–$1,500 per year

Step 4: Calculate Net Operating Income (NOI)

NOI is the most important number in your proforma:

NOI = Effective Gross Income – Total Operating Expenses

This number represents the property's income before debt service. It is what the lender uses to evaluate the deal, and it is the basis for the property's value in the income approach to appraisal.

Step 5: Calculate DSCR

The Debt Service Coverage Ratio tells the lender whether the property generates enough income to cover the mortgage payments:

DSCR = NOI ÷ Annual Debt Service (mortgage payments)

CMHC requires a minimum DSCR of 1.10x, meaning the property must produce at least 10% more income than the mortgage costs. Most lenders prefer to see 1.15x or higher for conventional deals. The higher your DSCR, the stronger your application.

Step 6: Cap Rate Analysis

The capitalization rate helps you evaluate whether the purchase price is reasonable:

Cap Rate = NOI ÷ Purchase Price

In Montreal, multifamily cap rates currently range from 4% to 6% depending on location and building quality. South Shore and Laval properties may offer slightly higher cap rates. A lower cap rate means you are paying more per dollar of income.

What Lenders Look For

Lenders want to see conservative, defensible assumptions. They will compare your projected rents to market data, verify your expense ratios against industry benchmarks, and stress-test your numbers at higher interest rates. The most common reasons proformas get rejected:

  • Rents above market without justification
  • Missing expense categories
  • Vacancy allowance below 3%
  • No management fee even if self-managing

5% Down on a 5-Plex: How CMHC Makes It Possible

The Conventional Problem

If you want to buy a multifamily property with conventional financing, most lenders require a 20% to 25% down payment. On a $1,000,000 five-plex, that means you need $200,000 to $250,000 in cash just for the down payment — before closing costs, inspections, and reserves.

For most investors, especially those building their first portfolio, that kind of capital requirement is a barrier. It slows down acquisition timelines and limits how many properties you can purchase.

How CMHC Changes the Math

CMHC mortgage loan insurance allows you to purchase a multifamily property (5+ units) with as little as 5% down. On that same $1,000,000 five-plex, your down payment drops to $50,000.

The Numbers Side by Side:

Conventional (20% down): $200,000 down payment on a $1M property
CMHC-insured (5% down): $50,000 down payment on the same $1M property

That is $150,000 in freed-up capital — enough for a down payment on two or three additional properties.

The Insurance Premium Trade-Off

CMHC insurance is not free. You pay a premium based on your loan-to-value ratio. At 95% LTV (5% down), the premium is at the higher end of the scale — typically around 4.00% to 6.25% of the loan amount depending on the amortization period and MLI Select scoring.

On a $950,000 mortgage (95% of $1M), that could mean a premium of $38,000 to $59,000. The good news: this premium gets added to the mortgage, so you do not pay it out of pocket. You pay it down gradually over the life of the loan.

Why the Premium Is Worth It

Three factors usually make the premium worthwhile:

  • Lower interest rate: CMHC-insured mortgages typically carry rates 50–150 basis points below conventional. On a $950,000 mortgage, a 1% rate difference saves you roughly $9,500 per year
  • Longer amortization: Access to 40–50 year amortization (versus 25–30 conventional) lowers your monthly payments significantly, improving cash flow
  • Capital efficiency: The money you did not spend on a larger down payment can be deployed elsewhere for higher returns

The Portfolio Scaling Strategy

This is where CMHC financing becomes a portfolio accelerator. Consider two investors, each starting with $200,000 in capital:

Investor A (conventional): Buys one $1M property with 20% down. Entire capital is deployed in a single asset.

Investor B (CMHC): Buys four $1M properties with 5% down ($50,000 each). Capital is spread across four assets generating four streams of rental income.

Investor B now controls $4,000,000 in real estate versus $1,000,000. If property values increase by 5%, Investor A gains $50,000 in equity. Investor B gains $200,000. The leverage effect is significant.

The Risks to Understand

Higher leverage is a double-edged sword. Important considerations:

  • Cash flow sensitivity: With 95% LTV, your mortgage payments are higher relative to income. Even small increases in vacancy or expenses can pressure your cash flow
  • Interest rate risk: At renewal, higher rates hit harder on larger mortgages. Stress-test your proforma at rates 2% above current levels
  • Property condition: CMHC has physical condition standards. Properties needing major repairs may not qualify
  • DSCR requirements: The property must meet CMHC's minimum 1.1x DSCR, which means not every property qualifies at 5% down

Who Should Use This Strategy?

The 5% down CMHC strategy works best for investors who:

  • Want to scale their portfolio faster than conventional financing allows
  • Are targeting properties that already cash flow positively (not value-add plays)
  • Understand the risks of leverage and have reserves for unexpected expenses
  • Are buying in markets with stable rental demand — Montreal, Laval, South Shore, and Quebec City all qualify

It is not the right strategy if you are buying a distressed property that needs heavy renovation, or if the property's current income does not meet DSCR requirements.

Getting Started

The first step is building a proforma to see if your target property qualifies for CMHC insurance. You need to know the NOI, the DSCR at current rates, and whether the property meets CMHC's physical and financial standards. A broker who specializes in multifamily financing can run these numbers with you and identify the best path forward.
